# Atlas Academy Doctrine - Immediate Classic-JRPG Feel

## Creative Direction

The Last Sword Protocol should immediately feel like entering an authored old-school JRPG world. This direction comes from the Production Director's stated inspiration and is supported by the comparative study in `academy/case-studies/comparative-jrpg-corpus-001.md`.

“Old JRPG” does not mean copying an old map, lowering resolution, or adding visual noise. It means preserving the spatial clarity, compact purpose, strong landmarks, and controlled curiosity visible across the selected NES/SNES corpus.

## The First-Screen Promise

Within the player's first view of a map, the composition should promise three things:

1. **I know what kind of place this is.**
2. **I can identify something worth moving toward.**
3. **I can imagine that the map contains something beyond what I see now.**

A map that provides only orientation feels sterile. A map that provides only mystery feels arbitrary. The classic-JRPG target is orientation plus curiosity.

## Required Player-Visible Qualities

### Readable place identity

Use a limited, dominant vocabulary for each area. A fishing village, shrine, cave, fortress, and forest path should not merely have different decoration; their routes, edges, focal objects, and room rhythms should read differently.

### Dominant landmark

Give every important map one anchor the player can name: the central well, broken tower, sealed door, glowing relay, bridge, shrine stair, market square, or cliff gate. Supporting landmarks may exist, but they should not compete equally with the anchor.

### Compact meaningful travel

Keep services, story nodes, and early decisions close enough that the player remains in a rhythm of intention. Longer travel must earn its duration through danger, anticipation, changing scenery, discovery, or narrative pressure.

### Legible boundaries

Players should usually understand collision before testing it. Terrain edges, wall faces, water, cliffs, vegetation density, furniture, counters, and elevation should communicate where movement is possible.

### Main path plus curiosity

The player should recognize the likely route without having every optional space explained. Side paths need a reason to exist: reward, shortcut, character moment, world detail, risk, or a visible deferred goal.

### Compression and release

Alternate confined connectors with rooms, squares, vistas, arenas, or landmark spaces. Use this rhythm to control tension and make destinations feel earned.

### Selective richness

Concentrate detail where it supports function, story, or navigation. Preserve calm movement space and avoid distributing equal visual weight across the whole map.

## Review Gate

A classic-JRPG claim does not pass review because the tiles are pixel art. A rendered map or playtest must demonstrate:

- a readable first view;
- one dominant landmark;
- an obvious main circulation structure;
- at least one meaningful curiosity hook;
- distinct compression and release;
- bounded, purposeful negative space;
- a limited area vocabulary;
- a memorable player-facing identity.

Record failures specifically. Examples: “the entry view exposes three equally weighted destinations and no dominant route,” or “the 18-second walk to the inn contains no decision, landmark change, encounter pressure, or story event.” Avoid unfalsifiable verdicts such as “does not feel retro enough.”
