## Observed Fact

- Official RPG Maker town and interior tutorials repeatedly show that counters, stairs, windows, pedestals, shelves, and roads should support, not confuse, a player's first read of the screen.[cite:16][cite:46]
- Official interior guidance reorganizes a poorly-composed shop into a coherent selling space with separated storage and private living area, demonstrating clustering by function directly.[cite:46]
- Official guidance warns against pointless oversized rooms that dilute purpose - negative space is only acceptable when it serves a purpose.[cite:46]
- Room zoning (public/semi-private/private) is presented as both an architectural reality and a gameplay affordance, letting players predict where services, clues, and rewards might plausibly appear.[cite:46][cite:49]

## Expert Opinion

- Horii's account of Dragon Quest's design favors simplified, legible systems over realism that becomes boring or annoying - a composition-adjacent principle in that it argues for compressing detail into readable form rather than maximal density.[cite:30]

## Community Practice

- Community opinion tends to push further than official guidance toward visual richness, elevation tricks, stronger scenic framing, and denser decoration, which can look attractive but can also drift toward clutter or prioritize screenshot aesthetics over play readability.[cite:21][cite:26] Per the source's own framing, this is weaker evidence than official guidance and should be treated as a style option, not a composition rule to enforce.[cite:16][cite:21][cite:26][cite:46]

## Research Hypothesis

- A focal point can be functional rather than scenic (an inn sign, a castle gate, a bridge, a staircase, a treasure alcove); semiotic clarity matters because props communicate meaning before interaction.[cite:49]
- Subtle empty-space structure can steer movement and improve navigation - negative space is an active composition tool, not merely unused area.[cite:37]
- Rhythm - alternating detail and calm - prevents both monotony and clutter, letting the player notice changes in function, tension, or story state.[cite:16][cite:37]
