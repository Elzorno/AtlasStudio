# The Last Sword Protocol - Game Vision

## High Concept

The Last Sword Protocol is a classic Dragon Quest-inspired JRPG about a young hero who discovers a mysterious sword that is not merely magical, but part of an ancient authentication and recovery system built by a lost civilization.

The game blends 1980s fantasy adventure energy with subtle real-world cybersecurity concepts hidden inside fantasy language.

## Target Feel

The player should feel like they are playing a modern love letter to early console JRPGs:

- Overworld exploration
- Towns with clues and personality
- Caves, towers, castles, shrines, and ruins
- Turn-based battles
- Simple but meaningful progression
- NPC hints that matter
- Secrets that reward curiosity
- A growing sense that the fantasy world is built on forgotten technology

## Primary Inspiration

Dragon Quest-style structure is the primary gameplay reference.

The world should not feel like a chain of Zelda-style screens. It should feel like an explorable JRPG world where the overworld connects locations and creates anticipation.

## Core Loop

```text
Explore overworld
  ↓
Find town, cave, dungeon, shrine, or castle
  ↓
Talk to NPCs and gather clues
  ↓
Enter dangerous location
  ↓
Fight, solve, discover, recover
  ↓
Gain item, spell, protocol key, or story unlock
  ↓
Open the next part of the world
```

## Cybersecurity Fantasy Layer

Cybersecurity concepts should be present, but subtle.

Examples:

- Authentication becomes worthiness or sword recognition.
- Access control becomes sealed gates or ancient locks.
- Code injection becomes corruptive forbidden magic.
- Backup recovery becomes restoring lost archives.
- Network routing becomes ancient relay paths.
- Malware becomes curses, worms, ghosts, or corrupted constructs.
- RFID or tokens become sigils, passes, medallions, or protocol keys.

The player should not feel like they are in a lecture. A cybersecurity professor should occasionally smile and think, "I know what that really is."

## Design Pillars

1. Classic JRPG readability.
2. Overworld-first exploration.
3. Towns and NPCs matter.
4. Dungeons teach mechanics gradually.
5. Cybersecurity concepts are embedded as fantasy metaphors.
6. The sword is both mythical artifact and ancient system interface.
7. Every region should have a local problem and a larger protocol implication.
8. The game should be buildable by AI agents under human direction.
